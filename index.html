BarberQ

A mobile app that lets walk-in customers check live queue status and estimated wait time at barber shops before leaving home.

Timeline
30 days
Role
Solo designer
Tools
Figma, Pen + Paper

01 — The Problem

The wait that costs you before you even arrive.

The Barber Shop Wait Time App helps walk-in customers in Tier 2 Indian cities check live queue status and estimated wait time at their barber shop before leaving home. It's built for people like Ansh — a 20-year-old student who visits his barber regularly but has no way of knowing the wait time without physically going there.

The problem this solves is simple: right now, finding out the wait time costs you the same time as the wait itself. This matters because in cities with limited good barbers, customers can't just switch shops — they wait, blindly, with no information.

02 — My Research

Ask about behaviour, not features.

I conducted 2 user interviews with people who regularly visit barber shops. I prepared structured questions focused on behavior and past experience rather than hypothetical feature requests. My goal was to understand how people currently deal with wait times and what coping mechanisms they've developed.

I also analyzed 2 competitor apps — Booksy and Unqueue — to understand the existing landscape. The key gap I found: no app combines live queue visibility with real-time wait estimation for walk-in customers.

03 — Key Insights

What the research revealed.

01People don't know the wait time without physically going there — the problem starts before they even leave home.
02Uncertainty is more painful than the actual wait — the app needs to make people feel informed and in control.
03People have built workarounds like going at off-peak times — the problem is real and consistent but no good solution exists.
04This problem is specific to Tier 2 cities where good barbers are limited — the solution needs to work for loyal, repeat customers.
05The real pain isn't the wait — it's not knowing when the wait will end.

04 — My Design Process

From problem to product.

Define
I created a user persona — Ansh, a 20-year-old student in Lucknow — based on insights from my user interviews. His behaviors, frustrations, and goals shaped every design decision that followed. From there I wrote a clear problem statement: Ansh needs a way to know the wait time at his barber shop before leaving home because right now he has to physically go there just to find out.
Ideate
I started with rapid paper sketches to explore layout ideas without the pressure of perfection. After settling on a structure I moved to grayscale wireframes in Figma — keeping color and typography out until the layout was solved.
Prototype
I connected all screens into a clickable prototype and tested it with a real user. Watching someone use it silently revealed a critical gap — there was no screen between the shop list and the queue. I added a Shop Detail screen to give users the information they needed before committing to join.
Design
Before touching hi-fi screens I built a design system — color styles, text styles, and reusable components. This kept all 5 screens visually consistent and saved significant time during hi-fi design.
Iterate
After external feedback I updated the border radius across all components and refined spacing. I also added a queue progress bar to the Queue View screen based on a user comment that the wait time should feel live — like a delivery tracker.

05 — Final Solution

5 screens. One clear problem solved.

The core customer flow works like this: the Home screen shows nearby barber shops with live wait times and distance at a glance. Tapping a shop opens the Shop Detail screen where Ansh sees the full picture — estimated wait, people ahead, barbers active, address, and opening hours. From there he taps Join Queue which takes him to the Queue View — where the wait time is the dominant element, displayed large and updated live. After confirming he lands on the Confirmation screen with his queue number, estimated wait, and a notification promise.

Unlike Booksy which only serves planned appointments, and Unqueue which shows walk-in options but goes silent after booking — BarberQ keeps Ansh informed from the moment he checks his phone to the moment he walks in.
